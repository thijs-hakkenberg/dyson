# Multi-Century Governance Structure: Literature Analysis

## Executive Summary

This analysis reviews recent contextual developments relevant to governance of multi-century, volunteer-driven space projects. **The key finding: there is no governance breakthrough applicable to Project Dyson's timescale and scope, but several 2025 developments provide useful building blocks.** NIST's in-space circular economy seminars address multi-generational governance frameworks. Multiple authors have identified a critical gap in international space law regarding autonomous self-replicating systems — the Artemis Accords legitimize resource extraction but are silent on self-replication. Borgue & Hein's quantitative self-replication estimates (~70% achievable near-term) directly inform governance requirements. This remains the most organizationally complex open question in the project.

---

## 1. NIST In-Space Circular Economy Governance

### 1.1 Second Seminar (August 2025)

NIST held its second seminar specifically addressing governance frameworks for multi-generational space industrial activities:

**Topics covered:**
- Public-private partnerships for space industrial development
- International coordination structures for shared infrastructure
- Standards and certification for in-space manufacturing
- Sustainability and resource management frameworks

### 1.2 Relevance to Project Dyson

The NIST framework addresses governance at the decade-to-generation timescale — shorter than Project Dyson's multi-century requirement but providing useful structural elements:
- Multi-stakeholder participation models
- Standards-based coordination (reducing reliance on centralized authority)
- Progressive governance (evolving rules as activities scale)

**Limitation:** NIST's framework assumes nation-state participation and government backing — neither of which Project Dyson can rely upon in its initial phases.

## 2. Self-Replication Governance Gap

### 2.1 Artemis Accords Analysis (2025)

Multiple 2025 authors have identified a critical gap in the international legal framework:

**What the Artemis Accords address:**
- Resource extraction and utilization rights
- Safety zones around operations
- Transparency and deconfliction
- Heritage site preservation

**What the Artemis Accords do NOT address:**
- Autonomous self-replicating systems
- Exponential growth of manufacturing capacity
- Governance of AI-directed resource allocation
- Long-term territorial implications of expanding autonomous systems

### 2.2 Proposed Treaty Elements

From the von Neumann probe governance analysis (Orbital Today, November 2025):

1. **Complete weaponisation ban** for self-replicating systems
2. **Verifiable replication cycle number limits** — caps on growth rate
3. **Strict target selection protocols** — what materials/bodies can be used
4. **Mandatory mission termination mechanisms** — kill switch requirements

### 2.3 Implications for Project Dyson

These proposed governance elements directly apply to Project Dyson's self-replicating foundry model:
- The project must proactively address weaponisation concerns before they become political barriers
- Replication rate limits must be designed into the system architecture, not imposed post-hoc
- Target selection (which asteroids to mine) needs a transparent, internationally accepted framework
- Mission termination capability is both a safety requirement and a governance trust-building tool

## 3. Borgue & Hein Self-Replication Estimates

### 3.1 Key Results

Borgue & Hein (Acta Astronautica, 2021) provide quantitative estimates for near-term self-replication:

- **~70% self-replication rate** achievable with current/near-term technologies for a sub-100 kg probe
- **Limiting factors**: AI capability, additive manufacturing precision, resource harvesting efficiency
- The remaining ~30% of mass requires Earth-sourced components (primarily electronics and specialized materials)

### 3.2 Relevance

This directly connects to Project Dyson's mass closure ratio targets:
- The 70% figure aligns with the project's assessment that electronics and semiconductors (rq-0-44) are the primary bottleneck
- Governance requirements scale with the self-replication rate — higher autonomy requires stronger governance frameworks
- The arXiv paper (2005.12303) provides the most rigorous quantitative analysis available

## 4. Application to Project Dyson

### 4.1 Governance Architecture Implications

The 2025 developments suggest a layered governance approach:

**Layer 1: Standards-based coordination (NIST model)**
- Technical standards for manufacturing processes
- Quality certification for in-space products
- Interoperability requirements for shared infrastructure
- Applicable to Phase 0 and early Phase 1

**Layer 2: Treaty-compatible safety framework (Artemis Accords extension)**
- Safety zones around mining and manufacturing operations
- Transparency and data sharing obligations
- Deconfliction with other space operators
- Applicable to Phase 1 expansion

**Layer 3: Self-replication governance (new framework needed)**
- Replication rate limits built into system architecture
- Resource allocation transparency
- Mission termination capability
- International oversight mechanism
- Required for Phase 2 and beyond

### 4.2 What Project Dyson Can Do Now

1. **Proactive engagement**: Participate in NIST circular economy discussions
2. **Design-in governance**: Build replication limits and termination mechanisms into the foundry architecture from Phase 0
3. **Transparency by default**: Publish all manufacturing capabilities and limitations openly
4. **Legal preparation**: Establish multi-jurisdictional legal structure (foundation model) that can evolve into treaty organization

## 5. Updated Assessment

### 5.1 Confidence Level: Low

Governance for multi-century autonomous space systems is an entirely unprecedented challenge. Available frameworks address decades, not centuries. No historical precedent exists for voluntary governance of exponentially growing autonomous systems.

### 5.2 Status: Remains Open

While the contextual developments provide useful building blocks, no purpose-built governance framework exists for Project Dyson's requirements. The question remains open pending dedicated governance design work.

### 5.3 What Is Emerging

- Governance frameworks for multi-generational space industry (NIST)
- Recognition of self-replication governance gap in international law
- Quantitative self-replication estimates informing governance requirements
- Proposed treaty elements for autonomous systems

### 5.4 What Remains Unaddressed

- Multi-century succession mechanisms for non-state organizations
- Governance of exponential growth of autonomous manufacturing capacity
- International legitimacy without state backing
- Transition governance from volunteer project to industrial entity
- Resolution of tension between open development and dual-use concerns

---

## 6. Research Gaps

1. **Governance simulation**: Model governance transitions across 10+ generations of leadership using different organizational structures (foundation, DAO, cooperative, hybrid)

2. **Self-replication governance framework**: Design specific governance mechanisms for self-replicating manufacturing systems, including rate limits, oversight, and termination

3. **Multi-jurisdictional legal structure**: Draft specific legal architectures for Project Dyson across multiple jurisdictions (Swiss association, Delaware corp, etc.)

4. **Stakeholder engagement**: Engage with NIST, UN COPUOS, and Artemis Accords signatory nations on governance requirements for autonomous space manufacturing

5. **Historical analysis**: Study the longest-lived non-state organizations (1000+ years) to extract governance mechanisms applicable to Project Dyson's timescale
